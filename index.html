Wiggle Script: Call for Participation

AKA

Please perform a wiggle script of your liking.

Record your wiggle with a phone camera, and submit it for use in a forthcoming digital artwork by Laura McCoy, commissioned through AKA Artist-Run Centre and curated by Lucas Regazzi.

1

A pose and some moves like a champion or goddess.

The right knee bends and the toe points. Your shoulders take turns lifting and releasing while your hands flip and your head rolls.

2

Like a pop in the hole, to the hole.

Make two onion shapes: one with your legs and one with your arms, one on top of the other. For two beats hold this pose, then switch to quick punches directly into the bottom onion.

3

Follow the arm it knows what's best.

Keep your left arm cradled in front of your stomach while your right arm swings around the body a little too fast, cutting through the air in the front and then the back, over and over, with the hips moving to the right each time the arm retreats.

4

Acknowledge the sky.

Move as though you are a tree trying to talk to god. Cross your legs and keep them crossed for the wiggle's duration.

What is a wiggle script?

A wiggle script at its most basic is a short prompt to move around. Specifically, a wiggle script acts like a recipe for release.1 If "it's hard to dance with a devil on your back," then a wiggle script facilitates some dancing "/ so shake him off."2

There are four here to choose from, each with a short set of instructions and a video.

Take a minute to let loose what you're holding on to. Expand any thinking around the 'way' a 'body' 'moves'. Any understanding of the prompt is good. Any movement that arrives is a welcome truth.

Performing a wiggle script changes the moment; the context is thwarted by the prompt, to make room for something messier. It's not a given that we wiggle in the kitchen, for example, but we can, and we should. There's no preferred site to perform a wiggle script, partially because no specific sites for it exist.

The urge to make wiggle scripts is necessity. There's a lot to grieve and not a lot of space to do it. Few folks are around to do it with.

If we all move a little bit—extend our toes, breathe fast or slow, two-step—to the same prompt for movement, at the same or different times, is that a message? Are we connecting?

We think so.

Lisa Robertson sometimes needs a flower machine.3 Our hearts need a wiggle meme to survive a little better. Community is an agreed upon action.
